A review of the 1st half for the cloud division
In H1 FY17, we picked up from where we left off last fiscal year, continuing our strong growth trajectory and the continuing evolution of offerings to match changing customer needs. We exited last fiscal year having doubled sales YoY and remained on a similar trend-line through the first half of FY16-17 ( April- September). Our strength in the public cloud and continued strong Enterprise uptake in OpenStack and the broader scale-out space were the factors driving that growth. We also maintained our attention to service delivery and quality recording customer renewal rates well over 90%.
OpenStack
Things were mixed in the OpenStack market as a number of players either exited or downsized their presence. Customer response to OpenStack visibly shifted towards a point of view that we have made consistently over the past 24 months - that OpenStack has to get simpler and more focused on the basics. Customers want to first deliver a base cloud - network, compute, storage - cleanly and cost-effectively and our investment to date has been in bringing that maturity to the platform and the tooling. In H1 we saw customers asking harder questions of OpenStack, something we welcomed. Distributed software - or Big Software as we call it - needs a model-driven approach to tooling and we did a better job of articulating that message than in prior periods. OpenStack remains a strong proposition when those needs are met.
Ubuntu everywhere
We also saw customers asking for a more holistic view of solutions across all of their scale-out needs - public & private clouds, containers, batch jobs, at-scale provisioning, HPC, intelligent devices on the network edge and more. The sum total of these shifts caused sales cycles to both lengthen and require a much broader set of answers. And those should lead to better answers and choices as well. Canonical has strong presence across much of that emerging landscape - from public cloud to devices on the edge, Ubuntu has >70% of OS share, and a significant amount of in-house expertise, customer and partner relationships. In H1 we started to leverage that breadth and bring those insights to customers in the form of up-front envisioning workshops designed to give customers a plan and roadmap for tackling scale-out in advance of jumping in.
Containers
Containers got hotter. Ubuntu continued to be the default platform for customers running containers and we engaged with a number of organisations now looking at that problem at greater scale. We released our Kubernetes offering, giving customers a supported, pure upstream answer to container management. And in Ubuntu 16.04 LTS, we providing the option of a 'machine' container - LXD - as a lighter/faster rip-and-replace alternative to VMs.
Public Cloud
On the public cloud we continued our deep technical collaboration on Ubuntu with both leading and emerging players - AWS, Azure, Google, Oracle and many more - focused on ensuring that the best of those platforms is exposed in Ubuntu and that it remains the best choice of OS for customers on the public cloud.

In summary, we see scale-out eating the datacenter. We continue to believe that scale-out requires a different infrastructure platform and a different class of tooling. We remain focused on maintaining our momentum in this space.
Primary talking points this quarter
Canonical expands container portfolio with supported distribution of Kubernetes
Canonical this week launched its own distribution of Kubernetes, with enterprise support, across a range of public clouds and private infrastructure. The Canonical Distribution of Kubernetes enables enterprise customers to operate and scale Kubernetes clusters on demand, anywhere. Leveraging Canonical’s existing Juju Charm eco-system, the Canonical Distribution of Kubernetes adds extensive operational and support tooling but is otherwise a perfectly standard Kubernetes experience, tracking upstream releases closely.
Canonical certifies Big Software solutions in Facebook’s new lab
Facebook announced the grand opening of its new hardware lab in Menlo Park to validate and certify software solutions and Canonical was one of the first to test its solutions are OCP compliant. At the new lab, enterprise and carrier-grade Big Software solutions were deployed including OpenStack Mitaka and Ubuntu Storage on Open Compute Project (OCP) Leopard, Honey Badger, and Knox. These solutions were deployed to bare metal in minutes and hours instead of days or weeks due to the use of two key technologies: Juju and MAAS, both of which are tested and validated at Facebook’s new facilities.
